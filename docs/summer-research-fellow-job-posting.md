## About the Project

The US Anti-Corruption Court is a novel civic-legal institution designed to address a gap in the
existing justice system: **corruption that is blatant and harmful but not clearly criminal under
existing statute**. The court is citizen-initiated, citizen-funded via signature-weighted escrow,
and adjudicated by a standing pool of 1,000+ qualified judges with per-stage random rotation — a
structural defense against the capture, bias, and dismantling patterns documented across decades
of international anti-corruption institutions.

The project is currently in the design and simulation phase. We are building a public website, a
formal whitepaper, and a discrete-event simulation of the court's operations modeled as a
**networked queuing system controlled by a Markov Decision Process (MDP)**. The simulation
treats cases as jobs, judges as servers, and uses reinforcement learning to optimize routing,
staffing, and prioritization decisions under real-world constraints.

We are looking for a **curious, mathematically oriented graduate student** to join us for the
summer and contribute meaningfully across several workstreams.

## What You'll Work On

### 1. Website Development

- Contribute to the project's public-facing Astro website (HTML/CSS/TypeScript)
- Improve site content, structure, and presentation of the court's design and mission
- Help build interactive pages that communicate the MDP modeling and simulation results to
  a non-technical audience

### 2. Whitepaper and Written Research

- Co-author and refine sections of the project's formal whitepaper, including the court plan,
  historical precedent analysis, and the MDP/queuing-control specification
- Research and document historical and contemporary anti-corruption institutions (ICAC, KPK,
  CICIG, Lava Jato, etc.) and extract structural lessons for the court's design
- Write clear, precise prose that bridges legal, mathematical, and policy audiences

### 3. Mathematical Modeling (MDPs and Queuing Theory)

- Work with the existing MDP formulation: state spaces, action spaces, reward structures,
  and policy evaluation for the court's multi-stage case-flow system
- Help formalize and extend the queuing-network model (Jackson-network-style topology with
  MDP-controlled routing, admission, and server-pool sizing)
- Contribute to the discrete-event simulation: agent-based modeling of individual cases,
  judges, sponsors, and defendants
- Explore policy evaluation techniques: offline policy evaluation (OPE), importance-weighted
  returns, counterfactual analysis, and stress-testing under adversarial conditions

### 4. Corruption Taxonomy and Classification

- Help develop a rigorous taxonomy that categorizes corruption into **combatable** vs.
  **non-combatable** categories — distinguishing corruption patterns that the court's
  mechanisms can meaningfully address from those that require different institutional
  responses
- Analyze archetype cases (regulatory capture, pension self-dealing, patent-system abuse,
  revolving-door rewards, insider dealing, etc.) and classify them along dimensions of
  detectability, evidence availability, diffuseness of harm, and amenability to
  non-criminal remedy
- Contribute to the court's living case-study catalog

### 5. Academic Program Development

- Research and propose new **university programs in corruption studies** — potential
  curricula, certificate programs, or interdisciplinary concentrations that could be housed
  in law schools, public policy programs, or applied mathematics departments
- Survey existing academic offerings in anti-corruption, institutional design, and
  computational social science to identify gaps and opportunities
- Draft outreach materials for academic partnerships

### 6. Programming and Simulation

- Write simulation code (TypeScript) for the court's discrete-event engine
- Build tooling for policy comparison, scenario analysis, and visualization of simulation
  outputs
- Contribute to the broader codebase as needed — data pipelines, analysis scripts,
  documentation tooling
